title: Out of Sight, Out of Mind&#58; Why AI Agents Instantly Forget Their Own Plans
---



Imagine a chef preparing a five-course dinner. A human chef reads the menu once, internalizes the plan, and carries that sequence in their head as they move around the kitchen. Now imagine a chef with a severe form of short-term amnesia: they must look at the physical recipe card before every single action. If someone sneaks in and removes the card, the chef immediately freezes, entirely forgetting whether they were about to boil pasta or chop onions.

According to a new study from Snowflake AI Research, today’s state-of-the-art Large Language Model (LLM) agents behave exactly like this forgetful chef. 

The paper, titled *"Plans Don’t Persist: Why Context Management Is Load Bearing for LLM Agents,"* reveals that AI agents do not internalize their own multi-step plans into a persistent mental state. Instead, they rely on constantly re-reading the text of their plans from their active chat history, or "context window." 

To prove this, researchers Aman Mehta and Anupam Datta developed a diagnostic technique called "replay pairing." They ran AI agents through virtual tasks under two conditions: one where the generated plan remained in the chat history, and an identical run where the plan was silently erased. By comparing the mathematical "brain activity" (hidden states) of the model between the two runs, they could track how long the memory of the plan persisted.

The results were stark. On Llama-3.1-70B executing chores in a simulated household environment called ALFWorld, the trace of the plan in the model's active memory spiked immediately after it was written, but plummeted 4.1-fold after just a single action. Within five steps, the plan's representation in the hidden state practically vanished. In another dataset, HotpotQA, the signal decayed even faster—by 12.4-fold in one step. 

The researchers also investigated newer "reasoning" models, like DeepSeek-R1. These models initially appeared to maintain their plans better, but the researchers discovered a clever illusion: the models were simply re-stating or re-deriving their plans step-by-step inside their hidden `<think>` blocks. Once these thinking traces were stripped away, the same fundamental amnesia was revealed. 

This finding exposes a major vulnerability for AI developers. Because LLM context windows are computationally expensive to maintain, engineers routinely use "context management" systems to compress, summarize, or delete older parts of a conversation to save memory. 

Since plans are written at the very beginning of a task, they are usually the first tokens to be evicted when memory gets full. 

To test the real-world stakes, the researchers subjected Llama-3.1 to a context-compression stress test. When the AI’s memory was naively trimmed to only keep the most recent messages, its success rate in the household simulator collapsed from 56.7% to a dismal 22%. 

Surprisingly, simply "pinning" the plan so it could never be deleted did not fix the problem. The researchers found that evicting other recent context to make room for the plan starved the agent of the immediate details it needed to operate. 

The takeaway for the AI industry is clear: context management is not just a background optimization trick; it is a load-bearing pillar of AI capability. If we want AI agents to successfully navigate long-horizon tasks, we cannot assume they "know" what they are doing. We must carefully guard the text they rely on to remember.
