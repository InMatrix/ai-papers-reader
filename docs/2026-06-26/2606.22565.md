title: Why Smart AI Models Struggle to "See" the Big Picture

The rise of "slow-thinking" artificial intelligence models, such as OpenAI’s o1 and DeepSeek-R1, has popularized a technique called Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning. By prompting AI to think step-by-step before delivering an answer, these models have achieved stunning breakthroughs in complex subjects like mathematics and coding. 

However, a groundbreaking new study from researchers at the Chinese Academy of Sciences reveals that when AI is asked to reason using both text and images, this "slow thinking" approach is far from a magic bullet. The paper, titled *"Look Light, Think Heavy: What Multimodal Chain-of-Thought Reasoning Can and Cannot Do,"* warns that for many visual tasks, CoT is not a free lunch. In fact, overthinking can actually make the AI perform worse.

## The Overthinking Penalty

To understand where CoT succeeds and fails, the researchers evaluated several state-of-the-art multimodal models across 12 different tasks. They divided these tasks into two categories: **perception** (basic seeing and locating) and **reasoning** (solving complex, multi-step problems).

The researchers discovered a stark divide. For complex reasoning tasks—such as solving a physics diagram or comparing data across multiple maps—CoT consistently boosted performance. But for simpler perception tasks, forcing the AI to write out an internal monologue backfired. 

Consider the task of **object counting**. If you show an AI an image of toy cars and ask, "How many items are in the image?", a direct answer is highly accurate. But when forced to use CoT, the model gets bogged down in unnecessary, winding descriptions of every car, leading to a nearly 5% drop in accuracy. Similarly, in **visual grounding**—such as drawing a box around a "pink shirt" in a photo—the extra reasoning steps simply distracted the AI, causing it to lose track of the object's precise location.

## "Look Light, Think Heavy"

Why does step-by-step thinking fail when images are involved? The researchers identified a fundamental bottleneck they call the **"Look Light, Think Heavy"** pattern. 

As an AI reasons through a problem, it decomposes its thoughts into "verbal reflection" (re-evaluating its logical steps) and "visual reflection" (re-examining the image). By analyzing the models' internal attention weights, the researchers found that as the AI’s internal monologue grows longer, it pays progressively less attention to the actual image. 

Imagine trying to solve a geometry puzzle. To find a missing angle, you first need to look at the diagram and visually recognize that two triangles are identical (visual reasoning). Once you know that, you can calculate the algebra (textual reasoning). The researchers found that AI is excellent at the math part, but terrible at the initial visual recognition. On custom-designed visual probes, model performance dropped by 20% compared to text-based probes. The AI simply stops "looking" at the diagram midway through its calculation, relying instead on its own generated text.

## Rebalancing Sight and Thought

The study also highlights a limitation in current open-source reasoning models. Because many are trained heavily on mathematics using reinforcement learning, they overemphasize algebraic equations while neglecting broader visual reasoning. 

To overcome this visual bottleneck, the authors point toward promising new frontiers, such as the "think-with-image" paradigm. Future models must learn to actively interact with visual data—for example, by zooming in on obscure parts of an image or using external code to analyze pixels—rather than just talking themselves into a corner. Until then, AI developers must learn to let their models look light, but think just right.
