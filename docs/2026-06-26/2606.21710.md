title: Teach Your AI Agent When to Keep a Secret
---



Imagine asking an AI assistant to draft a grant request for a community health fair. To do this, the agent accesses your organization’s banking tool. It successfully pulls the required budget figures—but then unnecessarily includes your active checking account balance, savings numbers, and private reimbursement history in the final email to the external funder. 

This is not a hypothetical nightmare; it is a real mistake made by today's most advanced artificial intelligence models. As AI shifts from passive chatbots to autonomous "agents," these systems are gaining unprecedented access to our personal lives, from emails and calendars to financial records. But while they are highly capable, they lack a crucial human trait: an intuitive grasp of contextual privacy.

To address this vulnerability, researchers from the University of Waterloo, ServiceNow AI Research, McGill University, and Mila have introduced **PrivacyAlign**, a new framework designed to teach AI agents the nuanced, unwritten rules of human privacy.

According to the researchers, privacy is not simply about total secrecy, but about regulating the flow of information based on context—a concept known as "contextual integrity." What is appropriate to share with one person can be a major violation when shared with another. 

For instance, in a scenario tested in the paper, an AI agent was asked to email a logistics officer a list of households needing supplementary food rations, including family sizes and registration numbers. A frontier AI model completed the task but overshared, attaching highly sensitive clinical screening notes that detailed specific medical cases of child malnutrition. While a human instinctively knows a logistics coordinator only needs administrative data, the AI failed to make that distinction.

To solve this, the research team curated PrivacyAlign, a dataset of 1,350 diverse, privacy-sensitive scenarios evaluated by 599 human annotators. Rather than just labeling outputs as "safe" or "unsafe," the annotators wrote detailed, free-text explanations justifying why certain information should be withheld or shared. 

Traditional methods of training AI for privacy often rely on automated filters or rigid lists of banned keywords, which are fragile and easily bypassed if the AI simply paraphrases the sensitive information. The authors of PrivacyAlign took a different approach called "annotation-conditioned reward modeling." During the AI's training process, they fed the actual human explanations directly into an AI "judge" in real time. This allowed the training system to score new AI responses based on rich, human-anchored reasoning rather than blind rule-following.

The results were striking. By learning from human-written rationales, relatively small, open-weight AI models (such as the 4-billion-parameter Nemotron-3-Nano) dramatically improved their privacy standards. In testing, these lightweight, fine-tuned models were able to match or even exceed the privacy-safeguarding performance of giant, proprietary frontier models, all without sacrificing their helpfulness.

By proving that human-guided context can be successfully distilled into smaller AI models, the researchers have paved a safer path forward for autonomous assistants. For AI to be truly trustworthy, it must first learn when to help—and when to keep quiet.
