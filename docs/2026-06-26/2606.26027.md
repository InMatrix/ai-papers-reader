title: Why AI Tool-Users Lose Their Minds Under Reinforcement Learning—and the "Tutor"
  That Saves Them
---



Imagine asking a virtual AI assistant to book you a business-class flight, check the price, and retrieve the invoice. To accomplish this, the artificial intelligence must execute a series of steps using external digital tools. Ideally, you want the AI to learn and improve this multi-step process autonomously through trial and error—a technique known as Reinforcement Learning (RL). However, when developers try this, they often hit a wall: the AI suddenly and catastrophically collapses, turning from a helpful assistant into a broken record spitting out empty, repetitive computer code.

A new paper by researchers at the Chinese Academy of Sciences exposes why this happens. In "Why Multi-Step Tool-Use Reinforcement Learning Collapses and How Supervisory Signals Fix It," the authors reveal that RL-trained agents do not actually lose their underlying reasoning abilities when they fail. Instead, they suffer from a "structural collapse."

To invoke an external tool like a flight-booking API, a Large Language Model (LLM) must format its output with extreme precision. For instance, it needs to output a valid sequence of formatting tags, known as control tokens, such as:

`<tool_call> {"name": "book_flight", "arguments": {"destination": "LAX"}} </tool_call><|im_end|>`

Under standard RL, the model explores different action paths to maximize its reward. But without strict structural guardrails, the mathematical optimization process accidentally over-amplifies the probability of those formatting tags. Slowly, the model begins to suffer from "text pollution," inserting fragments of code into natural-language responses. Eventually, the model's output degenerates entirely into a useless, minimal loop: `<tool_call><|im_end|>`. It has forgotten *how to speak* to the tool, even though its internal "logical brain" remains intact.

To prevent this formatting decay, the researchers systematically tested various ways of injecting "supervisory signals" back into the RL training loop. Simply feeding the model high-quality demonstration data beforehand (Supervised Fine-Tuning, or SFT) wasn't enough; the models still collapsed later or struggled to generalize when faced with unfamiliar tools.

Instead, the breakthrough lay in interleaving correction stages directly into the RL process. The most effective approach proposed by the authors is **Process Reflection Supervision (PRS)**. 

Think of standard RL as a driving instructor who only tells a student "pass" or "fail" at the very end of a road test. PRS, by contrast, is a talkative co-driver. When the model makes a mistake—such as trying to post a tweet before verifying the user's login credentials—an auxiliary "analyzer" AI steps in. It reviews the failure and writes a detailed textual analysis: *“The agent did not treat authentication as a required precondition... Do not call tools until the user supplies credentials.”* It then generates corrected, step-by-step examples for the model to learn from.

This logical scaffolding acts as a regularizer, preventing the AI from getting trapped in token-repetition loops. When tested on challenging benchmarks, models trained with this interleaved feedback achieved the highest performance scores, whereas raw RL yielded zero meaningful gains. 

The study’s takeaway is clear: as we build autonomous agents capable of navigating the digital world, raw trial-and-error is not enough. To prevent AI from collapsing under its own optimization pressure, we must teach them how to reflect on their mistakes.
