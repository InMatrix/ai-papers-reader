title: Why the 'Caveman' Prompting Trend is Secretly Bloating Your AI Bill
---



If you use artificial intelligence tools regularly, you have likely seen the advice: write like a caveman. Proponents of this "caveman style" suggest stripping your prompts to their bare bones—writing "Write email. Pitch product. Keep short." instead of a polite, grammatically correct instruction—to save money on API fees. 

But a new study by researchers at Adobe Research and independent scientists reveals that this popular trick is actually a financial trap. The paper, titled *CAVEWOMAN*, shows that compressing your inputs to an AI doesn’t save money—it actually hikes your bill while simultaneously wrecking the model's accuracy.

### The Cost of Confusion
Large Language Models (LLMs) charge users based on "tokens," which are essentially word fragments. Because output tokens (what the AI writes) are typically four to eight times more expensive than input tokens (what you write), true cost-efficiency depends on keeping the model's responses brief, not your questions. 

To test how models behave under a linguistic diet, the researchers built an evaluation framework called CAVEWOMAN. They tested eight prominent models, including GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4.6, and Gemma-4-E4B, across five benchmarks and five levels of grammatical reduction. 

The researchers split the tests into two methods: **input compression** (trimming the prompt before the AI sees it) and **output compression** (ordering the AI to reply using restricted grammar). 

For example, an unconstrained prompt about a math word problem might ask: *"Janet’s ducks lay 16 eggs per day. She eats three for breakfast... How much does she make?"* An extreme input compression might feed the AI: *"Janet ducks lay 16 eggs. eats three breakfast... dollars?"*

When researchers tried input compression, the results were a "strict lose-lose." Deprived of grammatical context, the AI models panicked. To compensate for the confusing prompt, they began writing much longer, rambling answers to try and reason through the mess. Because the AI was writing more words, the final API cost actually rose by up to 1.8 times on certain datasets, even as accuracy plummeted. 

Conversely, **output compression**—letting the AI read a full question but instructing it to reply in a "telegraphic" register (using only nouns, verbs, and numbers)—was a massive win, slashing realized costs by 1.4 to 2.4 times.

### The "Semantic Drift" Danger
However, saving money on output compression comes with a hidden risk: semantic drift. 

The researchers discovered that under output compression, accuracy decouples from the model's original reasoning. Across non-reasoning models, 51.9% of the compressed answers were technically correct (e.g., getting a "yes" or "no" right), but the text they generated no longer agreed with the reasoning of their unconstrained baselines. 

For instance, when asked if a specific theme park ride was a roller coaster, the unconstrained model reasoned: *"The passage states it is an 'indoor steel roller coaster'... which confirms it is."* Under telegraphic output compression, the model simply blurted out: *"Yes."* 

While "yes" is correct, the underlying chain of logic disappeared. If your business relies on reading the AI’s step-by-step reasoning for audits, transcripts, or safety checks, output compression effectively erases that value. 

The takeaway? Stop talking to AIs like cavemen. Give them rich, grammatically complete prompts, and if you must save money, programmatically restrict how they reply.
