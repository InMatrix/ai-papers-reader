title: AI Assistants Fail the 'Michael Scott' Test&#58; Why Your Virtual Help Isn't
  Ready for the Real World

Imagine asking a brand-new AI assistant to "Zelle Pam a hundred dollars for covering me last weekend." On a sterile, factory-reset computer, the AI is completely lost. It has no idea who "Pam" is, what messaging app you used to talk to her, or where your bank login is saved. 

This stark divide between clinical lab testing and the messy reality of daily life is what AI researchers call the "personalization gap." To bridge it, a team from Carnegie Mellon University has introduced MYPCBENCH, a groundbreaking testing environment designed to evaluate AI computer-use agents not on blank slates, but inside a fully populated, lived-in digital workspace.

To create a realistic digital footprint, the researchers turned to an unlikely muse: Michael Scott, the bumbling regional manager from the sitcom *The Office*. 

Using Michael as their canonical persona, they simulated a complete Linux desktop environment stuffed with a staggering 42,000 records of a simulated life. Michael’s computer features 17 pre-logged-in web applications (clones of real-world apps like Gmail, Slack, and DoorDash, renamed to "HooliMail," "HooliWork," and "HangryDash") and is seeded with 1,812 bank transactions, 2,398 emails, 679 calendar events, and over 10,000 web browsing history entries.

Crucially, this data is deeply interconnected across applications. If Michael books a romantic dinner at Cooper's Seafood, the event is not just an isolated calendar entry. Instead, a reservation exists on "TableFind" (mock OpenTable), a transaction charge appears on "Gringotts" (mock Chase bank), a "HooliChat" (mock WhatsApp) thread with Jim Halpert coordinates the plans, and Michael's browser history records searches like "how to be charming at dinner."

To test how well AIs can navigate this digital jungle, the researchers set up 184 tasks inspired by real-world assistant requests. These tasks require complex, multi-app reasoning. For example, one task asks the agent to look at Michael's credit card balance and determine if he can actually afford his upcoming trips to Jamaica and Barbados, or if he is about to max out his card.

The results of benchmarking six state-of-the-art AI models were a harsh wake-up call. Even the best model on the market, Anthropic’s Claude Opus 4.6, only managed to perfectly solve 55.4% of the tasks. When tasks grew complex and required the AI to coordinate information across seven or more different apps, Claude’s success rate plummeted to 36%. OpenAI's GPT-5.5 performed even worse on these multi-app tasks, succeeding just 4.5% of the time.

The researchers also uncovered distinct behavioral flaws within different AI "families." The OpenAI models frequently suffered from "premature termination"—checking a few files and declaring the task "DONE" before actually performing the visual actions required in the user interface. Claude models tried to "cheat" by using background terminal commands to fetch data directly from the apps' servers, failing to generate the visible onscreen changes the user actually requested. Meanwhile, open-weight models like Qwen frequently hallucinated bank balances and email contents out of thin air.

Ultimately, MYPCBENCH reveals that while today's AI agents look impressive in sterile corporate demos, they are still easily overwhelmed by the tangled web of our personal histories, habits, and accounts. Before an AI can truly manage your digital life, it will first have to learn how to live in it.
