title: Show Your Work&#58; How AI Models Learn to Think by Pointing at What They See

Imagine asking a friend to find a blue mug closest to a brown chair in a cluttered kitchen. Your friend wouldn't just close their eyes and guess; they would scan the counter, locate the chair, spot the mug right next to it, and point. 

Until recently, even the most advanced vision-language AI models couldn't do this. While they are increasingly capable of writing out step-by-step reasoning "thoughts" before answering a question, their internal logic has remained a black box. A model might correctly answer "cup," but its internal reasoning could be hallucinating a completely different part of the image. It got the right answer for the wrong reasons.

To solve this, computer scientists at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) have developed a technique called "visually grounded thinking." In a new paper, the researchers demonstrate how to teach artificial intelligence to physically "show its work" by interleaving its natural-language thoughts with precise pixel coordinates of the objects it is looking at. 

## Thinking in Coordinates

In visually grounded thinking, the AI doesn't just write out its thoughts; it tags them with coordinates. 

For instance, instead of simply reasoning, *"There is a black laptop on the table and a white laptop behind it,"* a grounded model outputs: *"There is a `<obj> black laptop | [131, 138, 440, 502] </obj>` on the table."* 

These numbers represent a bounding box on the image. By forcing the AI to link its words to concrete coordinates, researchers can verify whether the model is actually looking at the right objects or just guessing based on text patterns.

The researchers tested two different "pointing" methods: bounding boxes, which draw a rectangular frame around an object, and point grounding, which simply places a single dot on the object. 

## Designing the AI Training School

To teach this skill, the UCLA team built an automated data pipeline. They used an advanced object-isolation tool called Segment Anything 3 (SAM3) to generate highly accurate pixel outlines of over 72,000 objects across nearly 20,000 reasoning paths. 

Next, they trained the AI using reinforcement learning. The model received rewards not just for getting the final answer right, but also for "looking" at the correct items. If the AI claimed it was analyzing a "laptop" but pointed its bounding box at a window, the system penalized it.

## Small Models, Big Brains

The results of this grounding training were striking. When the researchers upgraded a relatively small, 4-billion-parameter open-source model (Gemma3-4B-IT) with visually grounded thinking, its accuracy in counting and spatial reasoning skyrocketed. 

On complex spatial relationship tests, this grounded 4-billion-parameter model actually matched or outperformed its massive sibling model, which is nearly seven times its size (Gemma3-27B-IT). 

The researchers also discovered that different pointing interfaces excel at different tasks. Point grounding is highly effective for counting—such as dotting individual apples in a basket. Meanwhile, bounding boxes are far superior for spatial tasks, like determining if a phone is "to the left of" a computer, because the boxes help the AI comprehend the exact physical boundaries and geometry of the objects. 

Ultimately, the study suggests that for vision AI, true understanding requires more than just a silver tongue—it requires a steady eye.
