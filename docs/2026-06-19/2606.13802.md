title: The Next Autocomplete Frontier Is the Humble Spreadsheet
---



While software developers have quickly grown accustomed to AI assistants writing entire blocks of code for them, the billions of office workers using spreadsheets have largely been left behind. Preparing a visually polished spreadsheet still requires a tedious ballet of manual labor: selecting ranges, choosing number formats, adjusting alignments, and repeatedly clicking formatting buttons. 

Now, researchers have taken a major step toward bringing "modeless" autocomplete to spreadsheets. In a new paper, a team of computer scientists has introduced the first comprehensive benchmark and framework designed to evaluate AI systems that predict a user’s next spreadsheet actions. 

To build this tool, the researchers had to overcome a major hurdle: unlike code platforms like GitHub, which preserve rich edit histories, spreadsheets are usually shared only in their final, static forms. To bridge this gap, the team meticulously reconstructed the creation histories of 52 real-world spreadsheets. Using a combination of large language models (LLMs) and human validation, they mapped out 11,907 individual, natural human steps—such as entering data, merging cells, and changing font styles.

### Simulating Human Frustration

What makes this research unique is how it evaluates AI. Instead of using static "offline" tests that merely check if a model can guess the very next step, the researchers designed an "online" simulation. This framework mimics how a real human interacts with an AI assistant.

Imagine you are building a monthly expense report. You have just typed "January" into cell A1. A smart assistant immediately suggests a sequence of three next steps: 
1. Auto-fill "February" through "December" across the row.
2. Bold the entire header row.
3. Highlight the "Total" column in bright yellow.

You like the first two suggestions, but the yellow highlight is a mistake—your company style guide requires blue. Under the new evaluation framework, if you "accept" the AI's suggestion, the simulation dynamically updates. It automatically crosses off the auto-fill and bolding tasks from your future to-do list, but it also adds a new "undo" action to clear the incorrect yellow highlight. 

By measuring the net "User Actions Saved" (UAS), the benchmark calculates whether the AI actually saved you effort, or if fixing its mistakes made you less productive.

### Shifting the Paradigm: Small and Silent

The researchers tested several AI models on this benchmark, revealing crucial insights for the future of office software. 

First, size isn't everything. While a massive, state-of-the-art model like GPT-5 saved users roughly 27% of their manual actions, a highly specialized, fine-tuned "small" language model (SmolLM2 with just 360 million parameters) achieved the exact same 27% savings. Because smaller models are cheaper and faster, they are much better suited to run locally on a user's laptop.

Second, the study proved that for AI assistants, silence is golden. An AI that aggressively suggests actions at every single turn without filtering itself actually reduced user productivity, resulting in a negative savings score of -19%. Because users had to spend so much time correcting or undoing bad guesses, they would have been faster working entirely by hand. 

Ultimately, the benchmark shows that the most successful spreadsheet copilots won't be the chatty ones that require constant prompting. Instead, the future belongs to quiet assistants that watch your workflow, spot repetitive patterns, and step in with high confidence only when they can truly save you a click.
