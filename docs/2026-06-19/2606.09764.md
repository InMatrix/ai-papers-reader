Our smartphones are not blank slates. They are cluttered, deeply personal archives of our lives, holding years of bank transactions, flight receipts, group chats, and fitness logs. Yet, when today's artificial intelligence "agents" are tested on mobile tasks, they are typically dropped into sterile, empty simulators with no history or identity. 

To bridge this gap, researchers from Carnegie Mellon University have introduced **iOSWorld**, the first interactive benchmark designed to evaluate how well AI agents can navigate a phone with a persistent, highly connected user identity.

To make the test realistic, the researchers built a fictional persona: Jordan Avery, a 31-year-old automation engineer living in San Francisco who is training for a half marathon. They populated 26 custom-built iOS applications with Jordan’s digital life. Because these apps share a single database, Jordan's world is realistically interconnected. For example, a food delivery order placed on *QuickBite* automatically triggers a corresponding credit card charge in *MyBank* and a receipt in *Mail*.

This rich environment allows iOSWorld to test agents on 133 tasks of varying complexity. While a simple single-app task might ask an AI to book a restaurant table, the benchmark's true test lies in multi-app and memory tasks. In one scenario, the AI is asked to look at Jordan’s ride-sharing app, deduce their most common commute route from past trips, and then book a ride along that route. In another, the agent must check a food order, verify the charge on their credit card statement, locate the receipt in their email inbox, and note any price discrepancies in their notes app.

The researchers tested leading AI models—including Anthropic's Claude 4.6, OpenAI's GPT-5.4, and Google's Gemini 3—under two setups. In the "vision-only" mode, the AI interacted with the phone just like a human, looking only at screenshots. In the "vision+XML" mode, the AI received privileged access to the phone's underlying accessibility code, which explicitly labels on-screen buttons and coordinates.

The results revealed that today's best AI is still far from being a reliable personal assistant. Even with privileged XML access, the top-performing model, Anthropic’s Claude Opus 4.6, only successfully completed 52% of the tasks overall—and scored a meager 37% on complex multi-app workflows. Without XML access, success rates plummeted; models routinely failed because of "iOS friction," such as missing tiny buttons or failing to navigate backward without a dedicated hardware back button.

Curiously, more information was not always better. While powerhouse models benefited from the XML data, smaller models like GPT-5.4 Mini actually performed *worse*. The massive influx of raw code—amounting to over 3,000 extra words of text per screen—easily overwhelmed their limited processing capacities, causing them to get stuck in infinite loops.

By open-sourcing iOSWorld, the researchers hope to push the AI community away from isolated, single-step tasks and toward "personally intelligent" systems. Until AI can understand who we are and piece together our fragmented digital lives, a truly helpful virtual companion will remain just out of reach.
