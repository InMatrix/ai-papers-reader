title: Can AI Run a Startup? New Benchmark Tests if Agents Can Play the Long Game
---



In recent years, artificial intelligence has gotten remarkably good at executing short, well-defined chores—like writing a single block of code or drafting a customer service reply. But can an AI agent successfully pilot an entire business through the chaotic, unpredictable waters of the real market? 

To find out, researchers from Princeton University have introduced **CEO-BENCH**, a grueling new testing ground that challenges language model agents to run a fictional subscription-software startup called "NovaMind" for 500 simulated days. 

Unlike typical AI evaluations that provide instant feedback, CEO-BENCH forces models to play the long game. Operating with a starting capital of $1 million, the AI must coordinate pricing, advertising, customer support, server capacity, and product research. To succeed, agents cannot rely on simple shortcuts. They must interact with a 19-table SQL database and execute actions through a programmable Python interface. 

The environment is designed to mimic the messy realities of entrepreneurship. For instance, if an agent decides to aggressively buy social media ads to acquire new customers, it might trigger a sudden demand surge. If the agent forgets to simultaneously upgrade its server infrastructure (its "capacity tier"), the system will experience outages. The customer satisfaction score will drop, leading to angry posts on a simulated social media feed, and—weeks later—a massive spike in subscription cancellations. 

The benchmark proved to be a brutal reality check for today's top AI models. Most tested agents quickly drove their startups into bankruptcy. Even highly capable models like Gemini 3 Flash and DeepSeek V4 Pro ran out of money mid-simulation. 

Only two models managed to consistently survive and grow their businesses beyond the initial $1 million starting balance: Anthropic’s Claude Opus 4.8 (reaching $27.8 million in its best run) and OpenAI’s GPT-5.5 (peaking at $21.3 million). Both managed to outperform a sophisticated, human-designed rule-based baseline that cleared $15.7 million. 

The true value of the study, however, lies in how these top-performing agents adapted. Rather than just blindly calling basic tools, the best models began writing their own software tools to manage the complexity. 

For example, on Day 77 of its run, Claude Opus 4.8 wrote a Python script to build its own cohort-based simulator. This custom program simulated future customer behavior under four different economic scenarios—ranging from a "disaster" scenario to an "optimistic" one—allowing the AI to forecast its cash runway before committing to expensive R&D projects. 

Similarly, on Day 133, GPT-5.5 wrote a script to mine its own database. By analyzing the JSON logs of past sales negotiations, the agent reconstructed the hidden price tolerances of its enterprise customers, allowing it to tailor future bids to what clients were actually willing to pay. 

In contrast, weaker or more cautious models failed to scale. Claude Opus 4.7 survived all 500 days but adopted an ultra-conservative "hibernate" strategy, cutting all ad spend and R&D to preserve cash, leaving it with a meager $389,959 at the end. 

The Princeton team notes that even the best models have immense room to grow; the theoretical upper limit of final cash in the simulator is $2.2 billion. CEO-BENCH marks a crucial shift in AI evaluation, moving from testing isolated tasks to measuring the sustained, strategic intelligence required to steer an organization through a changing world.
