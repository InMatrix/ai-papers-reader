title: AI’s "Deep Thinking" Trap&#58; New Training Method Restores Divergent Reasoning

In the race to make artificial intelligence more capable of complex reasoning, researchers have hit a paradoxical wall. While Reinforcement Learning (RL) has successfully taught Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to "think" longer and deeper, it has also made them surprisingly narrow-minded. A new paper from researchers at the Australian National University and their collaborators reveals that as AI models get better at following a single logical path, they lose the human-like ability to "try a different approach" when they get stuck.

The study identifies a phenomenon called "diversity collapse." Currently, the gold standard for training reasoning models is an algorithm called Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). While GRPO excels at refining a model's performance on a specific task, it inadvertently trains the AI to discard alternative strategies. It becomes a specialist that knows one way to solve a problem perfectly—but if that one way contains a subtle flaw, the AI is doomed to repeat the same mistake forever.

### The Breadth vs. Depth Trade-off

To build an intuition for this, the researchers compared "base" models (those without RL training) to their RL-enhanced counterparts. On paper, the RL models are more accurate. However, when the researchers gave the models multiple attempts to solve the same problem, the "lesser" base models actually outperformed the experts.

Consider a geometry problem asking for the height of a triangle. An RL-trained model typically defaults to a complex system of equations. If it makes a minor arithmetic error early on, it will repeat that error in every subsequent attempt because it only knows how to use equations. In contrast, the base model might try a different strategy on its second attempt, such as "validating" the multiple-choice options against the visual data. Even if its first attempt failed, its "divergent thinking" allowed it to find the exit through a different door.

Similarly, in object-counting tasks, RL models often get stuck in a tedious one-by-one enumeration. If they lose track, they fail. A base model, however, might switch to a "subtraction strategy"—counting the total number of objects and subtracting the ones that don't fit the criteria.

### Introducing MUPO: Brainstorming for AI

To fix this, the researchers developed Multi-Group Policy Optimization (MUPO). Instead of rewarding the single best response, MUPO partitions the model’s various attempts into different "strategy groups" based on how they are phrased and structured.

Think of it as a corporate brainstorming session. In standard RL, the loudest, most successful voice wins, and everyone else eventually just repeats what that person says. In MUPO, the "facilitator" (the algorithm) identifies three or four distinct schools of thought and rewards the best performers within *each* group. It also provides a "diversity reward" for groups that stay distinct from one another.

This approach prevents the model from converging on a single strategy too early in its training. By incentivizing the AI to maintain a repertoire of different reasoning pathways, the researchers created "MUPO-Thinker-7B." This model not only reached a new state-of-the-art in mathematical and general benchmarks but also showed a significantly higher success rate when given multiple chances to solve a problem.

The implications are significant for the next generation of AI. By ensuring that "all roads lead to Rome," MUPO suggests that the future of machine intelligence isn't just about thinking harder—it’s about being flexible enough to think differently.
