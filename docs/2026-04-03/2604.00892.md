Imagine you’ve asked a digital assistant to plan a complex driving route from downtown Pittsburgh to a local museum. Halfway through the process, as the agent is busy clicking through maps and calculating traffic, you realize you’re actually starting from a different neighborhood—Randyland—and shout out a correction. A human would immediately pivot. A state-of-the-art AI agent, however, might acknowledge your correction, keep the old route open, and finish the task using the wrong starting point anyway.

This "stubbornness" is a primary focus of a new paper from researchers at the University of Illinois Chicago and several other institutions. The study introduces **InterruptBench**, the first systematic benchmark designed to evaluate how Large Language Model (LLM) agents handle user interruptions during long-horizon web navigation tasks.

### The Challenge of "Long-Horizon" Tasks
As AI moves from answering simple questions to executing multi-step workflows—like managing a GitLab repository or navigating an e-commerce site—the "horizon" of the task grows. Over an hour-long task involving dozens of clicks and page loads, it is statistically likely that a human user will want to add a requirement, fix a typo, or retract a detail. 

The researchers found that while current LLMs are becoming better at following initial instructions, they struggle significantly when those instructions change mid-stream. To test this, they formalized three types of interruptions:

1.  **Addition:** Adding a new constraint (e.g., "I only need the top 3 products, not 5").
2.  **Revision:** Correcting an error (e.g., "I meant the Museum of Art, not the Natural History Museum").
3.  **Retraction:** Removing a previous detail (e.g., "Never mind about the expiry date, just get the token").

### The "Stale State" Trap
Through experiments with six powerful models—including the Claude 3.5 family, DeepSeek, and Mistral—the researchers identified a persistent bottleneck: "stale state" errors.

In a concrete example from the paper, an agent is tasked with comparing walking versus driving times. When the user interrupts to change the starting location, a "failing" agent treats the interruption as a surface-level message. It might say "Okay, I'll use Randyland," but it fails to actually go back to the browser's "From" field to delete the old address. It produces a final answer based on the original, now-incorrect data. 

In contrast, a successful agent treats the interruption as a "state-changing event." It recognizes that its previous progress is now "stale," navigates back to the input form, re-enters the data, and triggers a recomputation.

### Efficiency and the "Claude" Advantage
The study also measured "token overhead"—essentially the "thinking cost" of an interruption. They found that when users change their minds, models often expend massive amounts of internal deliberation (tokens) even if the number of physical clicks doesn't increase. 

The Claude family of models, particularly Claude 3.5 Opus, proved the most robust. While open-source models often plateaued or even regressed when faced with multiple turns of interruptions, the Claude models showed a "monotonic" improvement—meaning they actually got better at the task as more clarifying information was provided via interruptions.

### Why This Matters
For AI agents to move from experimental toys to reliable workplace tools, they must be "interruptible." The researchers conclude that simply being "smart" isn't enough; agents need better mechanisms for state tracking and "intent reconciliation." Until then, users might find that their most powerful AI assistants are also their most inflexible ones.
